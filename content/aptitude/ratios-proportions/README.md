# Ratios and Proportions

## Learning Objectives

- Understand what a ratio means (a:b) and how it compares two quantities
- Reduce and simplify ratios to their smallest integer form
- Solve proportions using cross-multiplication (a/b = c/d means a × d = b × c)
- Work with part-to-whole ratios
- Combine multiple ratios (e.g., if a:b and b:c, find a:b:c)
- Share amounts according to given ratios
- Find mean proportion and third proportional

## 1. What Is a Ratio?

A ratio compares two quantities by division. It tells you how much of one thing there is relative to another.

We write a ratio of a to b as a:b or a/b.

Example: In a jar with 15 red marbles and 10 blue marbles, the ratio of red to blue is 15:10.

Real-world example: A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar. The flour-to-sugar ratio is 2:3. If you double the recipe, it becomes 4:6, but the ratio 4:6 simplifies to 2:3 — the relationship stays the same.

Important: Ratios only make sense when the two quantities are in the same unit. Convert units first, then write the ratio.

## 2. Reducing and Simplifying Ratios

To reduce a ratio, divide both terms by their HCF.

Example: Reduce 48:72
- HCF of 48 and 72 is 24
- 48/24 = 2, 72/24 = 3
- Simplified ratio: 2:3

Example: Reduce 35:49
- HCF of 35 and 49 is 7
- 35/7 = 5, 49/7 = 7
- Simplified ratio: 5:7

## 3. Proportions and Cross-Multiplication

Two ratios a:b and c:d are in proportion if a/b = c/d.

The cross-multiplication rule: a/b = c/d means a × d = b × c.

This is incredibly useful because it lets you find any missing value.

Real-world example (Map scale): On a map, 2 cm represents 5 km. If two cities are 7.5 km apart, how far on the map?
- 2/x = 5/7.5 → 2 × 7.5 = 5x → x = 3 cm

Real-world example (Mixtures): Water and concentrate in ratio 5:2. If you have 14L concentrate, how much water?
- 14 corresponds to 2 parts → 1 part = 7L → Water = 5 × 7 = 35L

## 4. Part-to-Whole Ratios

When a quantity is divided into parts in a given ratio, the total parts equal the sum of all ratio terms.

Example: Boys:girls = 3:5 in a class of 48 students.
- Total parts = 3 + 5 = 8
- Each part = 48/8 = 6 students
- Boys = 3 × 6 = 18, Girls = 5 × 6 = 30

## 5. Combining Ratios

When you have two ratios sharing a common term, combine them into a single three-term ratio.

Rule: Make the common term the same in both ratios before combining.

Example: If a:b = 3:4 and b:c = 8:9, find a:b:c.
- Common term is b. LCM of 4 and 8 = 8.
- Multiply first ratio by 2: a:b = 6:8
- Keep second: b:c = 8:9
- Now b is 8 in both: a:b:c = 6:8:9

## 6. Sharing Amounts in Given Ratios

To divide an amount in a given ratio, divide by total parts, then multiply by each term.

Example: Share Rs 2,400 in ratio 5:3.
- Total parts = 5 + 3 = 8
- Each part = 2400/8 = Rs 300
- First: 5 × 300 = Rs 1,500, Second: 3 × 300 = Rs 900

## 7. Mean Proportion and Third Proportion

Mean Proportion (Geometric Mean): If a, b, c are in continued proportion (a:b = b:c), then b = √(a × c).

Third Proportion: If a:b = b:c, then c = b²/a.
